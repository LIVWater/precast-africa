Insights · The Demand Side

A continent urbanising in real time.

Vol. 03 Urbanisation, housing demand & the case for industrialised construction
I.  The arithmetic

Africa is urbanising at a pace no other region has ever matched. UN-DESA's World Urbanization Prospects estimates that Africa's urban population will roughly double between 2020 and 2050 — adding several hundred million city residents over a single human lifetime.

The implication is not abstract. It is, simply, a housing equation that cannot be solved at the current rate of site-built construction.

II.  The deficit

A housing deficit measured in tens of millions.

The Centre for Affordable Housing Finance in Africa (CAHF) and the African Development Bank have, across successive publications, placed the continental housing shortfall in the order of fifty million units — a number large enough that conventional construction industries, even at expanded pace, cannot close it within a generation.

The shortfall sits disproportionately in the affordable and entry-market segments — exactly the segments where unit price and programme certainty determine whether a project can be financed at all.

A building site, as a delivery method, has run out of room.
— Founding Position · Precast Africa
III.  Why industrialise

Industrialised construction changes what is possible.

The argument for industrialised construction is not new. Singapore mandates Prefabricated Prefinished Volumetric Construction (PPVC) on public housing above ten storeys. Sweden has built the majority of its single-family stock off-site for decades. The UK is rolling factory-built housing into the largest social programmes in a generation.

What is new is the continental case. The African deficit is larger, the price ceiling is lower, and the climate exposure of the workforce is higher than in any of those reference markets. Every advantage the factory offers — programme certainty, price stability, weatherproof quality, skills retention — Africa needs most.

IV.  Five forces

What is pulling the curve upward.

01 · Population

A younger continent, urbanising.

Africa's median age remains in the late teens. A growing share of that population is moving to cities for work, study, and services.

02 · Income

A rising middle.

Across most major African economies, household incomes are rising in real terms — particularly in the mid-market that mass housing serves.

03 · Migration

Internal & cross-border.

Rural-to-urban and inter-city migration is, in CAHF data, the single largest driver of urban housing demand on the continent.

04 · Backlog

Decades of under-delivery.

Most national housing programmes are running behind their own targets. The deficit compounds rather than closes.

05 · Climate

Pressure on long-life housing.

As temperatures rise and rainfall patterns shift, the durability and energy performance of new housing matters in a way it didn't a decade ago.

Colophon

The case is the numbers.

Industrialised construction is sometimes presented as a productivity argument. It is also, more importantly, a numbers argument. At the rate of demand the continent is generating, no other delivery method scales fast enough to matter.

The next decade will determine whether the housing pipeline is delivered as a series of weather-exposed building sites, or as a manufactured product. We are betting on the second.
