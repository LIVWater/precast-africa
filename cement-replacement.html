Manufacturing · Mix Design

Cement replacement.

Vol. 07Testing fly ash and slag mix designs against the same structural envelope.
I.  The question

The interesting question is not whether structural concrete can be made with less Portland clinker — the literature has been settled on that for decades. The interesting question is how much, in which application, with what testing programme, and at what point the durability curve starts to bend in the wrong direction.

Our mix testing programme is set up to answer exactly that, module type by module type.

II.  The two SCMs

Fly ash & GGBS — the workhorses.

Fly ash is a by-product of coal-fired power generation; ground granulated blast-furnace slag (GGBS) is a by-product of iron-making. Both are pozzolanic — they react with calcium hydroxide released by Portland clinker hydration to form additional binder. Both are permitted as cement substitutes in mainstream structural standards: SANS 50197 / EN 197 across South Africa and Europe, ASTM C595 / C1157 in the United States.

Typical substitution rates in structural mixes run 25–35% for fly ash and 30–50% for GGBS, with higher rates achievable for specific durability classes. Both reduce hydration heat — useful for thick precast sections — and improve long-term durability against chloride and sulphate attack.

The right substitution is not the highest one. It is the highest one that survives the test programme.
— On Materials · Precast Africa Studio
III.  The test programme

What the testing actually looks like.

Each candidate mix is taken through a graduated test sequence before it is approved for module production: fresh properties (slump, density), early-age strength (1, 3, 7 days), 28-day characteristic strength (the contractual reference), and durability indicators (water sorptivity, chloride penetration, and where relevant carbonation depth).

Mixes that pass all five gates go forward into trial casts at module scale, with cube samples taken from production batches and tested against the same envelope. Only mixes that hold their performance across both lab cubes and full-scale modules are added to the approved mix register.

IV.  Five gates

What every mix has to earn.

01 · Fresh

Workability for crane casting.

Slump within engineered band. Density within batch tolerance. Pumpability adequate for plant pour rates.

02 · Early strength

Programme-compatible.

1–7 day strength must support demoulding and handling cycles the plant programme depends on.

03 · 28-day

40 MPa, characteristic.

Mean of three cubes must meet engineered specification with the standard partial safety factor.

04 · Durability

Sorptivity & chloride.

Permeability indicators tested against SANS 3001-CO3-2 / EN 1992 service-life criteria.

05 · Trial cast

Module-scale verification.

Full-scale production cast with cubes taken from batch. Mix only enters the register if it passes both lab and production.

Colophon

Lower carbon, with the data behind it.

The numbers on our sustainability ledger come from the mix register, which comes from the test programme. None of it is rounded up for marketing.

Mix designs evolve as new SCMs become locally available — limestone calcined clay cement is a notable emerging candidate — but the gate criteria do not. Every new mix runs through the same five tests before it carries a single tonne of structural load.
