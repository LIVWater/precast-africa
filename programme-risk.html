Developers · Programme Risk

Cutting programme risk.

Vol. 11What PPVC removes from the developer's exposure — and what it does not.
I.  The exposure

Programme overrun is the most expensive thing that happens to a residential developer who is otherwise doing everything right. KPMG's Global Construction Survey series and McKinsey's Reinventing construction work have both, over many cycles, found that roughly two-thirds of major construction projects exceed planned schedule — and that the schedule overrun is, almost always, the largest single contributor to budget overrun.

Industrialised construction does not eliminate programme risk. It moves it.

II.  What we remove

The site exposure that simply ends.

Weather is the most obvious one. A site-built residential block in much of southern and eastern Africa loses 30–60 trade days per year to rain — and on the days work continues in rain, quality drops measurably. Trade availability is the second. Site-built construction is exposed to the local labour market in a way the factory is not.

Material supply is the third. Site-built construction needs cement, blocks, reinforcement, and finishes to arrive on the right day; modular construction needs them to arrive at the plant, which gives a longer buffer and a more predictable delivery window.

Programme certainty is not the absence of risk. It is the relocation of risk to the party who can absorb it.
— On Method · Precast Africa Studio
III.  What stays

What does not change.

PPVC does not remove the developer's planning and permitting risk. Approvals still take what they take. PPVC does not remove site-acquisition risk, or the foundation engineering — although it does compress the foundation programme because the structure above is lighter and faster to install.

And PPVC does not remove offtake risk. If the project is mis-priced for the market, no construction method recovers that. The honest version of programme risk transfer is: weather and trade exposure end at the plant gate. Everything else still requires good developer judgement.

IV.  Five things that change

What a PPVC programme actually delivers.

01 · Weather

No longer a critical-path input.

Manufacturing happens under a roof. Site installation is condensed enough that lift windows can usually be selected around forecasts.

02 · Trades

Permanent, not casual.

The trades that produce each module are factory-employed and trained on the system. The site only sees the install crew, which is smaller and more disciplined.

03 · Quality

Found in the plant, not the snag list.

Defects are caught at the QA station — where they cost a re-pour, not a damaged handover.

04 · Finance

A shorter interest window.

Compressed construction programme directly reduces the loan-interest cost on the developer's project finance.

05 · Disruption

Quieter neighbours.

Wet-trade exposure on site largely ends. Noise, dust, and traffic disruption around the site fall significantly — which matters for in-fill schemes and live community contexts.

Colophon

The programme is the product.

For a residential developer, programme is not a soft variable — it is the product. Investors price it. Lenders test it. Buyers expect it. PPVC does not eliminate programme risk; it reassigns the controllable parts of it to a manufacturer whose business model is built around delivering them.

That is the real argument for industrialised construction. The risk does not vanish. It moves to the party best equipped to absorb it.
