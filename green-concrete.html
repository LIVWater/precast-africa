Materials · Sustainability

Lower-carbon concrete, without compromise.

Vol. 06Green concrete — what the words actually mean on the plant floor.
I.  The problem

Cement is responsible for roughly seven to eight per cent of global CO₂ emissions, according to the IEA and the GCCA — most of it from the calcination of limestone in clinker production. A construction company that does not address its concrete is, simply, not addressing its carbon.

The honest version of "green concrete" is not a marketing line. It is a set of specific, well-tested engineering decisions about what goes into the mix.

II.  The lever

Cement replacement is the single biggest lever.

Supplementary cementitious materials — ground granulated blast-furnace slag (GGBS), fly ash, calcined clays, silica fume — can substitute a meaningful share of Portland clinker in a structural mix while maintaining (and often improving) durability. South African and EN-aligned standards already permit substitution levels of 30–50% in many structural applications, and emerging research on limestone calcined clay cement (LC³) extends the range.

The carbon arithmetic follows the substitution. Replacing a tonne of clinker with a tonne of slag or fly ash removes the calcination emissions associated with that tonne — typically 700–900 kg CO₂ per tonne of clinker avoided.

The carbon problem in concrete is, mostly, a cement problem. Solving it is mostly a cement replacement problem.
— On Materials · Precast Africa Studio
III.  The factory advantage

Why this is easier in a plant.

High-substitution mixes need disciplined batching, controlled curing, and tight QA. On a site, all three are difficult. In a plant, all three are routine. The factory is the only environment in residential construction where the developer can be honest about its concrete and have the test data to prove it.

That is why our reductions are operational consequences, not aspirations. Precision batching reduces over-pour. Closed-loop curing protects strength gain. Quality is verified at the casting bed, not at handover.

IV.  Five tests

What every "green" claim should survive.

01 · Cement Index

Carbon per m³, disclosed.

kgCO₂e per cubic metre of finished concrete, computed against an industry-standard EPD methodology — not back-calculated from finished-building totals.

02 · Substitution rate

The share that is not clinker.

The percentage of cementitious content replaced by SCMs (fly ash, slag, calcined clay, etc.) — reported by mix design.

03 · Strength to spec

40 MPa, verified.

Cube tests at 7 and 28 days against the engineered specification. No carbon reduction is acceptable that compromises rated strength.

04 · Durability

50-year structural design life.

Mix durability is verified to SANS 10100 / EN 206 service-life criteria — chloride ingress, carbonation, freeze-thaw where relevant.

05 · Third-party

External certification.

Embodied carbon is verified annually against an independent assessor. Self-reporting is not enough.

Colophon

Lower carbon is engineering, not branding.

A low-carbon concrete claim that does not name its mix design, its substitution rate, its testing standard, and its third-party verifier is a claim that has not been tested. The work, on this front, is engineering, and the proof is in the cube test.

We will only ever talk about concrete we can show. The mix designs, the substitution rates, the durability data, the EPDs — they are all part of the developer-partnership disclosure package, reported quarterly.
