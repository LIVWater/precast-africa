Manifesto

On the homes we owe each other.

Vol. 01 A letter to those building Africa's pipeline
I.  The position

A house can be measured. A home cannot.

We design and manufacture buildings — but the work, when we are honest about it, is something else. It is about the people who will turn a key, place a kettle, hang a photograph. It is about families who have been waiting longer than they should have. It is about time given back to the people who live with the consequence of how slowly we build.

II.  On time

Time is the one resource we cannot return.

A construction programme that runs three months over budget is a line item. To the family waiting, it is a third winter in a place they had planned to leave.

We treat speed not as marketing, but as a moral position. The faster a building can be honestly delivered, the more life it gives back to the people who need it.

The walls we build today will outlive the people who designed them, the people who poured them, and the policies that approved them. That is the weight of the work.
— Precast Africa
III.  On quality

Quality is dignity, rendered in concrete.

A cracked ceiling is not a defect. It is a daily reminder, for the person who lives beneath it, that they were not worth the second visit.

We build in a controlled environment because variability — the small inconsistencies of weather, fatigue, and goodwill — has a human cost we are not willing to externalise. The discipline of the factory is, finally, an act of care.

IV.  On dignity

Scale, done with care, is not anonymity.

The argument against mass housing has always been that scale erodes character — that what is delivered in volume is delivered carelessly. We disagree, and we are building the proof.

Every module is somebody's first home, last home, daughter's room. The repeatability of our system is what allows us to attend to that fact in detail, every time.

V.  What we hold to

Five quiet commitments. Held without fanfare.

01

We will not confuse motion with progress.

Programme certainty is honesty with people who are planning their lives against our dates.

02

We will not externalise the cost of variability.

If the weather is poor on site, that is our problem to solve, not theirs to live with.

03

We will design for the life inside.

The dimensions, light, and finishes serve the day that begins inside the room — not the rendering that sold it.

04

We will say what we will not do.

There are projects we are right for, and projects we are not. Being clear about the difference is part of the discipline.

05

We will outlast the trend cycle.

A building that lasts fifty years is a quiet vote of confidence in the people who will inherit it.

VI.  On legacy

What we leave behind will speak for us.

Buildings outlast political cycles, design trends, and the people who commissioned them. They are the most patient, most unforgiving record of what we believed about each other.

We are building for a continent whose housing pipeline cannot be finished in our lifetime — and whose first homes, when they are finally delivered, will need to stand long enough to be inherited.

Colophon

The work, as we understand it.

We are not the only people building homes. We are not the fastest, nor the cheapest, nor — in many cases — the loudest. We are, we hope, the most careful: about the lives that will be lived inside the rooms we cast, and about the conditions under which they will be delivered.

If that matches the way you think about your projects, we would like to hear from you.
