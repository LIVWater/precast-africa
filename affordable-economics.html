Insights · Economics

The economics of repeatable delivery.

Vol. 04Why the unit price holds when the unit is a product, not a project.
I.  The premise

Affordable housing economics are unforgiving. The price ceiling is set externally — by income bands, by mortgage criteria, by subsidy thresholds — and the developer's only real lever is the cost side. Conventional construction, with its weather, trade, and supply-chain exposure, treats every unit as a bespoke labour event. Industrialised construction does not.

When a home is manufactured rather than built, the cost curve behaves like a product cost curve. Volume bends it.

II.  The unit math

Three things volume actually does to unit cost.

First, fixed costs amortise. A precast plant is a capital asset; the more modules it produces in a year, the lower the per-unit share of plant overhead, depreciation, and supervision. McKinsey's Modular construction research has tracked this effect across European and Asian markets: above a threshold annual throughput, modular per-unit cost falls below in-situ.

Second, material yields improve. Precision batching eliminates the over-pour and breakage that show up routinely on site. Third, trade learning compounds — the tenth unit costs less to produce than the first, and the hundredth less again.

Affordability is not a discount applied to a finished home. It is a cost structure designed in from the start.
— On Method · Precast Africa Studio
III.  Programme as price

Time on site is money on the loan.

The cost the developer sees on a tender is rarely the cost the project lands at. Programme overrun absorbs developer profit in three places: interest on construction finance, holding cost on land, and delayed cash flow from first occupation. CAHF's affordable housing finance research repeatedly identifies these three as the dominant drivers of failed schemes.

PPVC compresses on-site programme dramatically — module installation runs to a published lift sequence, not a weather-exposed trade chain. The finance cost falls before the construction cost does, and the project becomes financeable on terms it could not otherwise reach.

IV.  Five cost levers

What repeatability actually changes.

01 · Materials

Precision batching, lower waste.

Concrete is dosed to the gram. Reinforcement is cut to programme. Off-cuts are recycled at the plant, not paid for twice.

02 · Labour

Permanent factory employment.

Skills compound rather than dispersing between sites. The tenth pour is faster, cleaner, and more accurate than the first.

03 · Finance

Compressed construction loan.

Faster site programmes shorten the interest window — often the single largest non-construction cost in affordable schemes.

04 · Defects

Caught in the plant, not in the home.

Quality issues are detected during manufacture, where they cost a re-pour. On a site, the same issue costs a defects list and a damaged handover.

05 · Programme

Fixed-date delivery.

The developer's pricing model can rely on the calendar. Weather, trade availability, and material delays are absorbed by the plant.

Colophon

Affordability is an operating model.

The affordable home that lands at a viable price is not a stripped version of a market home. It is the same home produced under a different operating model — one where repetition is rewarded, programme is bankable, and the unit price is set on the plant floor rather than negotiated on the building site.

That is the model PPVC enables. And it is the only model that scales to the housing equation the continent is being asked to solve.
