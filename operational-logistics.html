Delivery · Operations

1,000 units / year — the operational maths.

Vol. 13What it actually takes to run a 1,000-unit-per-year PPVC operation.
I.  The cadence

A thousand residential units per year is, very roughly, three units per working day. A typical residential unit uses 3–5 modules from the catalogue, so the plant cadence becomes 10–15 modules per working day. The arithmetic is unforgiving, but it is also achievable — provided every part of the operation is sized to it.

II.  The plant

The plant is the cadence.

A single casting bed turns over once per 24-hour cycle — concrete cast, cured, demoulded, and ready for the next pour. To hit 12 modules per working day, the plant needs 12 active beds running on stagger, plus a buffer. Reinforcement preparation, services pre-assembly, and finishing bays scale alongside the bed count.

The operational engineering — bed counts, crane reach, curing chamber capacity, transport bay slots — is sized at plant commissioning. Adding capacity later is a build project, not a setting.

A factory at scale is not a bigger factory. It is a factory with fewer surprises.
— On Method · Precast Africa Studio
III.  The transport

The truck is the warehouse.

Volumetric modules are large — wide, tall, heavy. Each finished module typically travels as an abnormal load, on a permitted route between plant and site. At a 10–15 module / day cadence, the operation runs 10–15 abnormal-load journeys daily, each scheduled to arrive within a tight installation window.

The discipline is just-in-time delivery: modules arrive in the lift sequence the crane needs, not in the order they were cast. Site storage is minimised — the crane picks the module off the truck and places it directly into position.

IV.  Five operational gates

What scale stops forgiving.

01 · Throughput

Bed turnover, every 24 hours.

Each casting bed must clear within the 24-hour cycle. A bed that runs late delays its own next pour and every subsequent station.

02 · QA capacity

Inspection scales with production.

The QA station's protocol cannot be compressed when production accelerates. We size QA personnel to the highest-cadence days, not the average.

03 · Transport

Permitted routes, pre-approved.

Abnormal-load permits, escort vehicles, and time-window agreements are arranged ahead of each project — not negotiated on the day.

04 · Crane planning

Lift sequence, published.

The site crane sequence is published in the project handover pack. Truck arrival is timed to the lift, not the other way around.

05 · People

Permanent crews, trained on the system.

Casting, QA, transport, and installation are run by permanent employees trained on this specific catalogue — not site-by-site casual labour.

Colophon

Scale is choreography.

One thousand homes a year is a number; the operational reality behind it is a daily choreography of beds, cures, trucks, and cranes. Each part of the operation depends on the others arriving on time, in the right state, in the right sequence.

The maths is unforgiving but tractable. The first plant proves the cadence. The second and third plants replicate the model into new regions — without redesigning the operation each time.
