The Honest Conversation

Fewer site workers. More permanent jobs.

Industrialised construction is a hard conversation if you only look at one side of it. On the site, you see fewer workers. In the factory and in the community, you see something very different, and far more durable.

Modular construction is often pitched on speed and quality. Both are true. But the first question that should come up with any large-scale industrialised housing programme, and rarely does, is the question about jobs. We think it deserves a direct answer. This is ours.

Set the frame

The honest version, stated plainly.

PPVC modular construction reduces on-site labour by approximately 75–85% per unit. This is not a risk to be hidden — it is a structural reality that must be planned for. Addressed correctly, it becomes a net positive: more permanent jobs, better pay, and higher skills.

For every 100 casual, unqualified site workers a conventional site would have engaged, the Precast Africa factory model creates 15–20 permanent, pensionable, CETA-qualified manufacturing employees — plus a wider ecosystem of learnership candidates, SMME sub-contractors, and community beneficiaries on top.

01
Factory Employment
Permanent, pensionable roles at the manufacturing plant — qualified artisans, operators, and engineers with formal employment contracts, UIF, and medical aid.
02
Skills Pipeline
CETA-accredited learnerships and QCTO trade qualifications — recruiting directly from communities within 30 km of the plant and project sites.
03
Enterprise Development
Formal ESD programme targeting black-owned SMMEs for sub-contracting of site preparation, logistics, fit-out, and community services — minimum 30% of eligible scope.
04
SED Investment
1% NPAT commitment to Socio-Economic Development in project-adjacent communities — infrastructure, education, and economic inclusion programmes.
The Numbers

What the shift actually changes

PPVC factory construction does three things to the employment profile of a residential programme. Each is auditable. None of them are optional.

On-site Labour Reduction per Unit
75–85%
Wet trades, brick-laying, plastering, and most finishing work shift to the factory — eliminating the largest category of casual, unqualified site workers, who are also the most economically vulnerable when a project ends.
Permanent Factory Jobs at 1,000 Units p.a.
300+
Permanent, pensionable factory roles: concrete batchers, mould operators, rebar fabricators, MEP installers, quality controllers, and plant supervisors — all on formal employment contracts with benefits.
Safety Incident Rate Reduction
60%
Factory environments vs construction sites. Controlled conditions, defined shifts, and consistent supervision cut reportable injuries by more than half compared with conventional site labour.
Conventional Site Workforce (per 100 units)
~100
Casual, project-based workers. Unqualified, no pension, no formal training, no fixed-term security. Employment ends when the project ends — typically with no transferable qualification.
PPVC Factory Workforce (equivalent)
~20
Permanent, qualified manufacturing employees. Lower headcount — but pensionable, CETA-qualified, and employed year-round across the full programme, not project to project.
Learnership Intake Target — Year 1
48
CETA-registered learners in the first cohort — drawn from communities within 30 km of the manufacturing plant and project sites. Targeting 60% black female intake across the programme.

The Framework

Four pillars. Measurable outcomes.

Reducing on-site labour is only useful if you build something better in its place. Every developer partnership we enter is structured against the same four pillars — each one tied to a quantified target, each one mapping cleanly onto B-BBEE scorecard elements under the Construction Sector Code.

300+
Pillar 1 — Factory Employment

Permanent manufacturing jobs at the Precast Africa plant, growing with programme volume. Formal contracts, pension, medical aid, and a structured career ladder from operator to shift supervisor to plant manager.

Skills Pipeline

From community to career — six pathways

Sourcing close, training deep, and promoting internally before advertising externally. The pipeline is how today's learner becomes tomorrow's plant manager.

01
Adult Basic Education
Where it begins

Where literacy and numeracy gaps exist, ABET is the first step. The intake commits to closing those gaps before any technical training begins — measured, scheduled, and pre-funded into the programme cost.

02
CETA Learnership
12–18 month cohort

NQF Level 2–4 learnership cohorts registered with CETA. Each learner is paired with a workplace supervisor. Stipend paid throughout. Drop-out is treated as a programme failure to investigate, not a learner failure to write off.

03
QCTO Trade Qualification
Recognised trades

Concrete manufacturing, plumbing, electrical, MEP — all leading to recognised QCTO trade qualifications. The certificate is portable; the learner owns it whether they stay with us or not.

04
Permanent Placement
Pipeline outcome

Completed learners receive priority placement into permanent factory roles — the same posts they trained alongside. The certificate is not the endpoint; the contract is.

05
Supervisor Pathway
Internal promotion first

Shift-supervisor and team-leader roles are advertised internally before externally. Demonstrated performance + competency-based assessment opens the next role. The career ladder is published, not implied.

06
Plant Manager
Long-game

The longer ambition: a plant manager who started as a learner. We build the bench depth from day one and we don't pretend the top role is unreachable — because the people in it once held the role next to it.

The Point of the Shift

Different jobs — permanent, qualified, structured to multiply.

Conventional construction has been the default employment route for unqualified labour in South Africa for a generation. It has also been the route with the worst safety record, the lowest skills retention, and the weakest career progression of any sector touching that demographic. Industrialised construction does not solve that on its own. But it does open the door to a different model — one where employment is permanent, qualifications are recognised, and the next role is already mapped.

That is the case for thinking about modular as a social proposition, not only a technical one. It is also the case for being deliberate about how the social side is built — because the upside does not happen automatically. It happens because the framework is written into the contract, reported quarterly, and audited annually. That is what we build with every developer we work with. Not the marketing version. The contractual version.
